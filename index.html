It wasn't just them

The Civil Rights Movement couldn't have had support from just one side. It needed both to move forward from a state which was disputed among the many.

Who were they?

There were many, many white people and organizations that backed blacks in the fight for civil rights, whether it be for the riddance of seing discrimination or the guilt that follows. Here are some of the contenders:

Juliette Hampton Morgan

Morgan, a librarian, didn't have a car in her city of Montgomery, so she had to ride the bus. Seeing the treatment of blacks on the bus, she took action by writing letters to The Montgomery Advertiser about the mistreatment she sees. Hatred by many surrounding passengers, co-workers, and bus drivers led to a cross being burnt on her lawn and the death of her by intentional drug overdose due to death threats.

Jim Letherer

Letherer (in the center with crutches), an amputee who lost his right leg to cancer, found the spirit and need to march to Selma in 1965. He led with Martin Luther King Jr. and kept protesters' spirits high by chanting "Left, left, left!"

The Impact

In Harper Lee's To Kill a Mockingbird , character stood out. Atticus Finch was known for his persistence helping Tom Robinson because he felt it was the moral thing to do. All the people listed and many others found ways to help join the end to mass racial discrimination. The Voting Rights Act of 1965 was passed due to the support of both blacks and whites. An investigation into the KKK was launched due to deaths of white people occurring. All the actions took wouldn't mean as much without people like Atticus Finch, because there really were people who did things like Atticus, and they went through the challenges that faced them.
